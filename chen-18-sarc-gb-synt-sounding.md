What's in a word: Sounding sarcastic in British English 
Aoju Chen and Lou Boves
2018 Journal of the International Phonetic Association Volume 48 Special Issue

# Abstract

* Using a simulated telephone conversation task, we elicited sarcastic
  * utterance types (i.e. declaratives, tag questions and wh-exclamatives from
    native speakers of the southern variety of British English.
* previous studies focus on static prosodic measurements at the utterance level
  (eg mean pitch, pitch span, intensity), 
  we examined both static prosodic measurements and 
  continuous changes in contour shape in the semantically most important words
  (or key words) of the sarcastic utterances and their counterparts in the
  sincere utterances. To this end, we adopted Functional Data Analysis to model
  pitch variation as contours and represent the contours as continuous function
  in statistical analysis. We found that 
* sarcasm and sincerity are prosodically distinguishable in the key words alone.
  * sarcastic utterances are realised with a longer duration and a flatter fall
  * regardless of utterance type and speaker gender. These results are
  * compatible with previous reports on the use of a smaller pitch span and a
    slower speech rate in sarcastic utterances than in sincere utterances in
    North American English. We also observed 
* notable differences in the use of 
  * minimum pitch, maximum pitch and contour shape in different utterance types
  * mean pitch and duration by male and female speakers.
* the prosody of the key words in sarcastic utterances and their counterparts in
  sincere utterances has yielded useful predictors for the presence (or absence)
  of sarcasm in an utterance.
